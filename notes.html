Medieval & Mughal Period Judicial System

Mughal Judicial Structure

Chief Judicial Officer: The official responsible for providing authoritative legal opinions during the Mughal period was called "Musti" or similar titles.
Final Authority: The Emperor was the final court of appeal in all matters of justice and the ultimate adjudicator in the judicial system.

Legal Basis in Medieval India

Hindu Laws: Based on principles found in the Vedas, Dharma Shatra, and Astha Satra.
Legal Representation: During medieval India, legal representatives were known as "Vakils" who represented parties in disputes.

Village Administration

Panchayats: In villages, panchayats primarily dealt with local disputes and administered justice based on customs and traditions.

British Era Legal Development

Foundation of Modern Judicial System

Charter of 1726: Established the Mayor's Court in the presidential towns, laying the foundation of the modern judicial system.
Regulating Act of 1773: A significant legal development that established regulatory frameworks for the colonial judicial system.

Supreme Court Establishment

Charter of 1774: The Supreme Court of Judicature at Calcutta was established under this charter, setting eligibility criteria for enrollment as advocates.
Eligibility Restriction: The right of enrollment as an advocate in the Supreme Court was initially restricted to English and Irish Barristers.

First Law Commission

Establishment: The First Law Commission was established in 1834. Chairman: Lord Macaulay headed the First Law Commission.

Indian High Courts Act, 1861

Courts Established: Abolished the Crown Court and Sadar Adalats, replacing them with High Courts that served as the highest court of appeal in their respective jurisdictions.

Legal Practitioners Act, 1879

Scope: Regulated advocates, pleaders, and legal practitioners during the British era, establishing the first formal framework for the legal profession.

Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926

Significance: Provided for the establishment of Bar Councils to regulate and manage the legal profession in India.

Modern Legal System

Federal Court of India

Establishment: The Federal Court was established to serve as the highest court of appeal during the later British period.

Independence Era Changes

Public Interest Litigation (PIL): The concept of PIL developed during the independent India period as a tool for social justice and public welfare.

Advocates Act 1961

Major Reform: The Advocates Act abolished the previous distinction between different classes of lawyers (barristers, solicitors, pleaders) and established a unified legal profession.

Advocates Act 1961 - Key Provisions

Fundamental Rights

Right to Practice (Section 33): Advocates have the absolute right to practice throughout the territory of India subject to professional conduct norms.
Right of Free Audience (Section 23): Advocates have the right to appear and conduct cases on behalf of clients in any court.
Order of Priority: Attorney General has highest priority, followed by Solicitor General, Additional Solicitor General, and then Senior Advocates.

Enrollment & Qualification

Minimum Age: A person must be at least 21 years old to be enrolled as an advocate.
Educational Qualification: Must possess a law degree from a recognized university. Foreign degrees must be recognized by the respective State Bar Council.
Enrollment Authority: State Bar Councils have the authority to enroll advocates in their respective jurisdictions.
Single Enrollment: A person can only be enrolled with one State Bar Council. They cannot be enrolled with multiple State Bar Councils simultaneously.

Disqualifications

Moral Turpitude: A person is disqualified from enrollment if convicted for an offense involving moral turpitude or professional misconduct.
Disqualification Period: After conviction and imprisonment, a person must wait for 2 years before becoming eligible for enrollment as an advocate.

Senior Advocate Designation

Criteria: Senior advocate designation is conferred based on merit and ability (Section 16).
Restrictions: Senior advocates cannot directly deal with clients and must file vakalat (power of attorney) through junior advocates. They must practice with the assistance of junior advocates.

Right to Practice

Scope: An advocate is entitled to practice before any court, criminal authority, or institution legally authorized to take evidence.
Non-Advocates (Section 32): Non-advocates may appear before courts only with prior permission of the court and cannot normally practice law.

Professional Restrictions

Conflict of Interest: An advocate should not accept a brief in a case where they are a witness or have a personal interest in the matter.
Simultaneous Business & Contingency Fees: An advocate cannot practice law and run a full-time business simultaneously, and cannot charge contingency fees.
Advertising: Advertising by advocates is permitted within the BCI rules and professional guidelines.

Seniority Determination

Primary Factor: The seniority of an advocate is primarily determined by their date of enrollment.
Same Date Resolution (Section 21): For advocates enrolled on the same date, seniority is determined by the time of enrollment in their respective courts.

Bar Council of India (BCI)

Structure & Authority

Definition: BCI is a statutory body defined as a body corporate with perpetual succession and common seal under the Advocates Act.
Headquarters: The Bar Council of India is headquartered in New Delhi.
Supervisory Role: BCI serves as the supervisory and appellate authority for all State Bar Councils.

Functions & Powers

Power to Make Rules (Section 15): BCI has the power to make rules for the regulation of the legal profession.
Power to Direct (Section 7): BCI has the power to give directions to State Bar Councils on matters of legal profession management and administration.
Funds: BCI can constitute one or more funds for the management of financial resources and professional development.

Legal Education

Recognition Power: BCI has the power to recognize universities and law colleges for legal education under Section 2.
Legal Education Committee: A mandatory committee of BCI that oversees legal education standards and quality assurance.
All India Bar Examination (AIBE): BCI conducts the AIBE after the judgment in the landmark case establishing its necessity for ensuring uniform standards.

Membership & Administration

Ex-officio Member: The Attorney General of India is the ex-officio member of the Bar Council of India.
Election of Leadership: Members of BCI elect the Chairman and Vice-Chairman among themselves for a specified period.
Secretary & Accountant: Appointed by the BCI to manage administrative and financial matters.

State Bar Councils

Establishment (Section 4): State Bar Councils are established under the Advocates Act in each state.
Ex-officio Member: The Advocate General of the state is the ex-officio member of the State Bar Council.
Electorate Requirement: If electorate exceeds 10,000 advocates, additional government members are included. If less than 5,000, different composition rules apply.
Term of Members: Elected members of State Bar Council serve a term of 5 years.
Seat Vacation: A member is deemed to have vacated their seat if absent from 3 consecutive meetings without sufficient cause.

Rolls Maintained

Two-Part Roll (Section 16): State Bar Councils maintain a roll of advocates in two parts: one for senior advocates and another for other advocates.
Copy to BCI (Section 19): State Bar Councils must send a copy of the rolls to the Council of India periodically.

Professional Ethics & Conduct

Core Duties

Officer of Court: An advocate is an officer of the court and member of a noble profession with special responsibilities.
Duty Towards Client: Maintain confidentiality, charge reasonable fees, assist justice, and serve client interests within legal boundaries.
Duty Towards Court: Maintain respect for the court, assist in administration of justice, and uphold integrity of legal proceedings.

Professional Conduct Rules

BCI Rules (Part 6): Professional conduct standards are outlined in Part 6 of the BCI Rules on professional conduct.
Confidentiality: Advocates must maintain strict confidentiality of client communications and information.
Fee Schedule: Bar Council of India Rule 49 prescribes rules for determining fees consistent with the nature and complexity of cases.
Contingent Fees Prohibition: Accepting contingent fees (fees based on case outcome percentage) is professionally improper and prohibited in India.

Professional Misconduct

Definition: Professional misconduct includes conduct directly related to legal practice, while other misconduct relates to offenses involving moral turpitude.
Prohibition on Dual Practice: An advocate cannot practice law and run a full-time business business simultaneously.
Strike Action: Strike by advocates was held to be illegal and unethical by the Supreme Court.
Without Environment: Practicing as an advocate without proper enrollment/environment is punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years.

Disciplinary Procedures

Disciplinary Committee

Composition: Consists of 3 members, including both advocates and external members with relevant experience.
Procedure: Conducts proceedings through summons, witness examination, evidence recording, and formal investigation.
Time Limit: Disciplinary proceedings must be concluded within 1 year. If not concluded, automatic transfer occurs.

Complaints & Investigation

Prima Facie Case: When complaint is received, the committee checks whether there is prima facie case before proceeding.
Referral to Committee: If prima facie case is found, the matter is referred to the disciplinary committee for full proceedings.

Punishments (Section 35)

Reprimand: Warning or censure of the advocate for minor misconduct.
Suspension: Temporary suspension from practice for a specified period as per the gravity of misconduct.
Removal: Removal of name from the roll of advocates for serious or repeated misconduct.

Appeal & Review

Appeal Period: Limitation period for filing an appeal to BCI against State Bar Council disciplinary order is 60 days.
Appellate Procedure: From State Bar Council Disciplinary Committee, appeal goes to BCI, and then to the Supreme Court.
Review (Section 44): Disciplinary committee can review its own order under specific circumstances.

Protection of BCI Actions

Section 48: Protects the Bar Council for actions taken in good faith, ensuring officials are not personally liable for professional decisions.
